It's Just Luck

Three games on the science of serendipity — why good luck scales with how much of the world you touch, why an unexpected find is worthless until it meets a question you're already carrying, and why chasing many small leads quietly beats waiting for one big break — after Christian Busch's The Serendipity Mindset.

Game One · The trigger

You can't connect a dot you never meet

Serendipity isn't a gift handed to the lucky. Christian Busch splits luck in two: blind luck — passive, it just happens to you — and smart luck — the kind you can work for. Smart luck starts with a trigger: an unexpected thing you bump into. And triggers are a numbers game. The more of the world you actually touch, the more of them cross your path.

Below, the field is a year, and the faint dots are unexpected events scattered through it. Your exposure decides how far you roam from your routine; your alertness is how wide a reach you bring. Press play to live the year — then turn exposure down to zero and watch what being clever buys you when you never leave the house.

Same alertness, different exposure: a wider path sweeps more of the field. Stay on one spot and even a sharp eye sees almost nothing.

This is the unglamorous half of luck. You can be the most perceptive person in the room, but if you keep to the same desk, the same five people, the same commute, you've shrunk the field to a dot. Lucky people aren't built differently — they're standing in more places. Busch calls this widening your surface area for the unexpected: more conversations, more side projects, more rooms you have no obvious reason to be in.

“…they were always making discoveries, by accidents and sagacity, of things which they were not in quest of.”

Horace Walpole · 1754, coining the word “serendipity”

Note the second word: sagacity. Accidents alone aren't enough — Walpole's princes were also sharp. Which is the catch waiting in Game Two: the field can be thick with triggers, and you can still walk straight past every one of them.

Game Two · Bisociation

An unexpected find is worthless until it meets a question

Here's the hinge of the whole thing. A trigger on its own is just noise — a stray fact, an odd remark, a result that didn't fit. It becomes serendipity only when it connects to something you were already carrying: a problem, a curiosity, an open question. Arthur Koestler named that collision bisociation — two unrelated frames snapping together. Busch's plainer phrase: connecting the dots.

On the left, observations rain in. On the right are the questions you happen to be carrying. Turn the questions off — let your mind just wander — and every observation lands as grey noise; you walked past all of them. Turn them on, and the ones that rhyme with a question light up. Add more questions and you catch more of what falls.

The same observations fall every time. What changes is whether anything in your head is ready to catch them.

This is why two people walk through the identical week and one calls it lucky. The trigger was there for both. Only one of them was holding a question it could answer. Penicillin, the microwave, Velcro, Post-it notes — none of them were the find anyone went looking for; each was an accident that landed on a prepared mind. Carrying a few live questions is the cheapest luck-amplifier there is. So is giving people more to connect you to — Busch's idea of dropping several small hooks in a conversation instead of one polished headline.

“In the fields of observation chance favours only the prepared mind.”

Louis Pasteur · 1854

So you've widened the field, and you're primed to notice. You're now generating more connections than you can possibly chase. Most of them will go nowhere. That's not a flaw — it's the whole shape of the payoff, and it's Game Three.

Game Three · Tenacity

Most leads fizzle — so chase enough of them

A connected dot is still only a lead. Busch's third stage is the least romantic and the most decisive: tenacity — actually doing the work to turn an accident into something real. And here serendipity wears the same shape as its two sibling essays. Each lead is a small, droppable bet: a little effort spent, walk away if it's nothing. Most return nothing. A rare one pays out of all proportion. So the math goes convex — your downside per lead is capped, your upside isn't — and the only way to reach the fat tail is to take enough shots.

Each dot is one possible life. Betting everything on the break that feels biggest is a single coin flip; chasing many small leads floors the loss and keeps the tail.

Watch the slider's left edge. Pursue zero leads and you're perfectly safe — and nothing ever happens. A connection you never act on is just a nice story you tell at dinner. This is the part people skip: they widen the field, they spot the trigger, they make the connection — and then they don't follow through, and conclude they were never lucky. Serendipity isn't having the leads. It's having the stubbornness to chase a stack of them, knowing most are duds, betting that one isn't.

“Serendipity is about smart luck.”

Christian Busch · The Serendipity Mindset
In one line

Stop waiting for luck. Build the conditions for it.

Widen the field so triggers cross your path. Carry live questions so you catch the ones that matter. Then have the tenacity to chase a stack of leads, knowing most won't pay. Surface area, a prepared mind, and follow-through — three dials, all of them yours to turn. The accident still has to happen. But you get to decide how often you're standing where it can find you. As the chemist said a century before anyone called it a mindset: chance favours the prepared.

“Serendipity is about active luck — it's about the luck we create for ourselves and how we imbue meaning into the unexpected.”

Christian Busch

The serendipity framework here — the trigger → bisociation → tenacity process, and “smart luck” versus “blind luck” — is Christian Busch's, from The Serendipity Mindset: The Art and Science of Creating Good Luck (2020) and his writing on connecting the dots. “Bisociation” is Arthur Koestler's, from The Act of Creation (1964). The historical anchors are real: Horace Walpole coined “serendipity” in a 1754 letter, after the fairy tale of the Three Princes of Serendip; Louis Pasteur's “prepared mind” is from an 1854 lecture. The convex bridge — that smart luck has the same payoff shape as a small, reversible bet — is my own synthesis, the thread tying this to its siblings, It's Just an Experiment (action) and It's Just Math (money). The interactive games are my toy models — deliberately simple, directionally honest.
